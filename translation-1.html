  ABSTRACT:

This is a critical study of the ḥadīth narrated by Suhayl b. Abī Ṣāliḥ, from his dad, from Abū Hurayrah: “don’t initiate the greeting of peace* (al-Salām) with polytheists”, which Muslim included in his Ṣaḥīḥ, and which had some differences in wording between its different narrators. It was also used in argumentation by those who maintained that it was categorically forbidden to initiate the greeting of peace with non-Muslims, even though there exists a number of other authentic ḥadīths that command spreading the greeting of peace. It may seem that there exists an apparent contradiction between them, which is the reason that led the present author to study this ḥadīth, and elucidate the differences that appeared in its different narrations, and resolving its conflict with the other ḥadīths on extending and spreading the greeting of peace and returning it back with non-Muslims, as mentioned in the Prophetic Sunnah. All of this with the objective that the present study fulfills all the needs in determining every authentic ḥadīths on this topic.

  INTRODUCTION:

All praise be to God, the Lord of all creation, and may peace and blessings be upon the Trustworthy Messenger, his family, his companions and all those who follow him:

This is a critical study of the ḥadīth narrated by Suhayl b. Abī Ṣāliḥ, from his dad, from Abū Hurayrah: “don’t initiate the greeting of peace with polytheists—and in another narration: Christians and Jews. And when you meet any of them in the road, then make him take its narrowest path.”

The need for knowing the ruling on such a matter, that causes much afflictions and problems throughout the community, is obvious considering that people are now mixed with each other to a degree unseen throughout our history, and Muslims spread across all countries, dealing and working with people from other religions, and maybe they were discomforted if they decided not to initiate the greeting, especially if the context required that, such as if they entered non-Muslims’ houses and worksplaces, as is evident in the ḥadīth of Suhayl. Furthermore. this ḥadīth contradicts the generality of the Qurʾānic and Prophetic texts that command spreading the greeting of peace between people. Ibn Ḥajar mentioned the differing of the Righteous Forebears (Salaf) on this issue due to the conflict between the apparent meaning of the texts, he said, “Forbidding the initiation of the greeting of peace with non-Muslims was explicitly mentioned in what was included by Muslim and al-Bukhārī in his al-Adab al-Mufrad, from a path via Suhayl b. Abī Ṣāliḥ, from his dad, from Abū Hurayrah, “don’t initiate the greeting of peace with Christians and Jews. And when you meet any of them in the road, then make him take its narrowest path.” ... And a group of scholars maintained that it was permissible to initiate the greeting of peace with them: al-Ṭabarī related from Ibn ʿUyaynah: ‘It’s permissible to initiate the greeting of peace with non-Muslims based on the verse, “God does not forbid you to deal kindly and justly with anyone who has not fought you...”, and Abraham’s statement to his [non-Muslim] dad, “Peace be with you”.’ After mentioning the ḥadīth of Abū Umāmah that he used to initiate the greeting of peace with everyone that he passed by, and when he was asked about that he replied, ‘God made peace the greeting of our community (ummah), and a safety for the [non-Muslim] people under our protection’, al-Bayhaqī noted, ‘that was the opinion of Abū Umāmah, and the ḥadīth of Abū Hurayrah on prohibiting its initiation is the correct view.’ [al-Qādī] ʿIyāḍ responded concerning the verse and the saying of Abraham—upon him be peace—to his dad that the purpose from that was distancing oneself and leaving [polytheists], the aim was not to offer a greeting. And some of the Salaf stated that the verse, “and say, ‘Peace’: they will come to know.” was abrogated by the verse on qitāl.”[1]

The clash between the evidence pointing out to its prohibition and the evidence that points to its permissibility, reached the point where those who argued for it being forbidden claimed the occurence of abrogation, others maintained that there was a restriction, and another group went to provide different interpretations of the verses. This is also what led to the disagreement between the Righteous Forebears (Salaf) from the time of the companions on this matter, mainly because of the apparent contradiction between the texts. As such, studying and knowing the correct meaning of this ḥadīth comes as a necessity, in such a way that removes this clash, and that makes it in agreement with the Qurʾānic verses as well as the Prophetic ḥadīths that have both more authentic chains of transmission, and are more explicit in meaning.

  II. TEXTS THAT CONTRADICT SUHAYL’S ḤADĪTH:

The apparent meaning of the ḥadīth of Suhayl is contradicted by a number of other more general and more explicit ḥadīths that command spreading the greeting of peace, and which have more authentic chains of transmission, among which:

  1. The ḥadīth of al-Barāʾ in the Ṣaḥīḥayn: “The Messenger of God ﷺ commanded us to do seven things: ... and spreading the greeting of peace.”[59]
  2. The ḥadīth of ʿAbd Allāh b. Salām, that the first thing that the Messenger of God ﷺ said in his first day in the city of al-Madīnah: “O People! Spread greetings of peace, feed the hungry, uphold the ties of kinship, be in prayer when others are asleep, and you’ll enter Paradise in peace.”[60]
  3. The ḥadīth of ʿAbd Allāh b. ʿAmr, that a man asked the Messenger of God ﷺ ‘Which quality of Islām is the best?’, he responded, “To feed the hungry and to greet with peace those you know and those you don’t know.”[61]

And these famous and authentic ḥadīths all convey the generality of the command, and they’re in perfect accordance with the apparent meaning of the Qurʾān, such as in this verse commanding with regards to polytheists to state: “Peace be with you! We don’t seek the company of ignorant people,”[62] and the verse: “but turn away from them and say, ‘Peace’.”[63] Ibn Jarīr al-Ṭabarī said interpreting the former, “‘Peace be with you!’ where salām (peace) is in the nominative case (= salāmun) by the pronoun ‘for you’ or ‘upon you’.”[64]

The ḥadīth of Suhayl contains a restriction to the generality of these texts, whose apparent meaning encompasses both generality and total inclusion. Supporting the case that it’s restricted would require an authentic and explicit text that indicates restriction. But the ḥadīth of Suhayl not only restricts, but also abrogates the ruling of those Qurʾānic verses that indicate the permissibility of initiating the greeting of peace with polytheists when turning away from them. The ḥadīth of Suhayl thus eliminated this ruling altogether, and prohibited from initiating the greeting of peace with polytheists and People of the Book. And this is the only evidence of those who argue for its prohibition and went as far as to claim abrogation, and they don’t have any other evidence besides it!

A number of people from the Righteous Forebears (Salaf) argued about the permissibility of initiating the greeting of peace with polytheists based on the generality of these verses, examples include:

  1. From Shuʿayb b. al-Ḥabḥāb, he said: I was with ʿAlī b. ʿAbd Allāh al-Bāriqīy when a Jew or Christian passed by, and [ʿAlī] greeted him with peace. Shuʿayb remarked: ‘​‌​‌He’s a Jew or Christian!’ [ʿAlī] responded by reciting to me the last of Sūrat al-Zukhruf, “The Prophet has said, ‘My Lord, truly these are people who don’t believe,’ but turn away from them and say, ‘Peace’: they will come to know.”[65].[66]
  2. From al-Masʿūdī, from ʿAwn b. ʿAbd Allāh, he said: Muḥammad b. Kaʿb asked ʿUmar b. ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz about initiating the greeting of peace with non-Muslim citizens, he responded: ‘You greet back, but you don’t initiate it.’ I then asked him about his personal opinion and he replied: ‘I don’t see any issue with initiating it with them!’ Upon asking him as to why, he responded by citing the verse, “but turn away from them and say, ‘Peace’: they will come to know.”[67]

Ziyād, he said: “I would take Abū Umāmah’s hand and accompany him to his house, and he would not pass by a Muslim, a Christian, a child, or an adult except that he would say to them: ‘Peace be upon you! Peace be upon you! Peace be upon you!’ When he would reach his house’s door, he would face us and state, ‘O son of my brother! Our Prophet ﷺ ordered us to spread greetings of peace’.”[71]
    • And in a narration from Muḥammad b. Ziyād al-Alhānī, from Abū Umāmah al-Bāhilīy, that he would initiate the greeting of peace with every person that he meets. He said: There was only one case in which someone else was the first [to initiate the greeting of peace], and it was a Jew who was hiding behind a cylinder and then suddenly came out and greeted him with peace. Abū Umāmah then asked him, ‘Woe to you Jew! What made you behave this way?’ He replied, ‘I saw that you were a man who would greet with peace a lot. So I knew that it was a favor, and I liked to take from it.’ Abū Umāmah then stated, ‘Woe to you! I heard the Messenger of God ﷺ say, “God made peace the greeting of our community (ummah), and a safety for the [non-Muslim] people under our protection”.’[72] However, the correct view is that this wording is attributed to Abū Umāmah, and it isn’t authentically narrated from the Prophet.
  2. ʿAbd Allāh b. Masʿūd:
    • From al-Aʿmash, from Ibrāhīm, from ʿAlqamah who said: “I went with ʿAbd Allāh (Ibn Masʿūd) who was accompagnied by some merchants (dahāqīn) from the town of al-Ḥīrah. When they reached al-Kūfah, they took on a different road. [Ibn Masʿūd] turned and saw them leaving, so he greeted them with peace. I then asked him: ‘Do you initiate the greeting of peace with these non-Muslims?’ He replied: ‘Yes! They accompanied me, and companionship has its right’.”[73]
    • And from Shuʿbah, from al-Mughīrah and Sulaymān al-Aʿmash, from Ibrāhīm, from ʿAlqamah: “I went with ʿAbd Allāh (meaning Ibn Masʿūd) on a donkey, and some people from the merchants accompagnied him. When they reached a bridge they took on a different path. ʿAbd Allāh then turned, and didn’t see anyone from them, so he asked: ‘Where are our companions?’ I replied that they had taken the other road. So ʿAbd Allāh said: ‘Peace be upon you!’ I asked: ‘Isn’t this disliked?’ He replied: ‘This is the right of companionship’.”[74]
    • And from al-Masʿūdiyī, from al-Qāsim, from Ibn ʿAbd al-Raḥmān, from Tamīm b. Salamah, who said: “Some polytheists went with ʿAbd Allāh [b. Masʿūd], and when he reached the gate of the palace he greeted them with peace.”[75]

    • From Shuʿbah, from Suhayl b. Abī Ṣāliḥ who said: I went with my dad to Syria, and we found that they would pass by the churches of Christians and that they would initiate the greeting of peace with them. My dad then said addressing them: ‘don’t initiate the greeting of peace with them, for Abū Hurayrah narrated to us that the Messenger of God ﷺ said, “don’t initiate the greeting of peace with them. And when you meet any of them in the road, then make him take its narrowest path”.’[79] And in another version of the ḥadīth: “don’t initiate the greeting of peace with them, and make them take its narrowest path.”[80]

This last narration shows that this position—on the permissibility of initiating the greeting of peace with the People of the Book—is a famous one for the successors from Syria (al-Shām), as well as Abū Umāmah and Abū al-Dardāʾ who also happened to live there.

Ibn ʿAbd al-Barr tried to take their actions as an indication of its permissibility, and not that it’s encouraged (mustaḥab). So he interpreted the ḥadīth “don’t initiate the greeting of peace with Jews” to mean: “It isn’t obligatory for you to initiate the greeting of peace with Jews.” He thus said, “It was narrated from a group of companions and successors—may God be pleased with them—that they used to initiate the greeting of peace with everyone they met, be it a Muslim or non-Muslim citizen. Its meaning, and God knows best, is that it’s not obligatory to initiate the greeting of peace for a Muslim who is passing by a sitting non-Muslim, or a Muslim who is riding to a walking non-Muslim, as is obligatory for Muslims toward each other by the Sunnah. However, if he does it then there’s no blame on him. It’s as if he ﷺ said, “It isn’t obligatory for you to initiate the greeting of peace with them.” The evidence for this is found in what is narrated by al-Walīd b. Muslim, from ʿUrwah b. Ruwaym, who said: “I saw Abū Umāmah al-Bāhilīy initiate the greeting of peace with everyone that he met, be it a Muslim or non-Muslim citizen. And he would say: ‘It’s the greeting of the people of our religion, and a safety for the [non-Muslim] people under our protection, and one of the Names of God that we spread among us.’ It isn’t the case that Abū Umāmah would contradict the Sunnah, if that’s authentically narrated to him, rather the meaning is as we interpreted, and God knows best. Additional evidence for those who held this opinion is found in the verse “God does not forbid you to deal kindly and justly with anyone who has not fought you ...”.”[81]

As you can see, he’s very explicit in interpreting the ḥadīth of Suhayl to not infer the prohibition of initiating the greeting of peace with non-Muslims, but rather with the meaning that initiating the greeting of peace with them isn’t obligatory but is permissible. And it’s evident how far-fetched this is, since it’s clear from the actions of Abū Umāmah that he would initiate the greeting of peace with them out of preference (istiḥbāb), acting upon the apparent meaning of the ḥadīth “he commanded us to spread the greeting of peace.” The actions of these companions in times of war and peace wouldn’t have been problematic to Ibn ʿAbd al-Barr had he went to the interpretation of Isḥāq b. Rāhawayh—as we shall see—where the implication of the ḥadīth of Suhayl is the prohibition of issuing a guarantee of safety (amān) to them in the case of war.

Some of the general ḥadīths that order spreading the greeting of peace contain many other commands, such as the ḥadīth “We were commanded to do seven things: ...” The exegetes and jurists argued about the generality of its wordings, with the exception of those that are restricted by another evidence, and among them: “O People! Spread greetings of peace, feed the hungry, uphold the ties of kinship.” And it’s known that upholding the ties of kinship is obligatory—even with non-Muslims, such as if one’s parents were non-Muslim. The same can be said with regards to feeding the hungry, no one held that it was restricted only to Muslims, rather it’s general even with non-Muslims, and it’s considered among the actions of kindness without any disagrement.

As al-Bayhaqī said: “We already mentioned in Kitāb al-Sunan the permission of spending on them by right of kinship when in need. As well as visiting them when they’re sick while hoping that they embrace Islām, and shrouding (takfīn) those who died from his kin. In addition to recompensating the one from which he took goods.”[82]

And al-Qurtubī said: “It’s agreed upon in this religion that it’s obligatory to uphold the ties of kinship, and that cutting them is forbidden. It has been authentically narrated from the Prophet ﷺ that he responded to Asmāʾ after she asked him: ‘Should I keep upholding my ties with my mother?’, ‘Yes, keep your ties with your mother’, so he ordered her to do that even though her mother was a non-Muslim. By her insistance [it’s understood] that keeping the ties of kinship with non-Muslims is also virtuous.”[83]

The same applies to the ‘we we’re commanded to do seven things’ ḥadīth, in which it was mentioned: “respond to the the sneezer (tashmīt al-ʿāṭis), help those who vow to fulfill it, help the oppressed, spread the greeting of peace, accept invitations, and follow funeral processions” and in a narration “and return the greeting of peace.”

And this is general, with other texts coming adding more insistence and care for Muslims with regards to fulfilling this right over their Muslim brother. And the general stays so, as long as there’s no evidence against it. As for initiating the greeting of peace, it’s evident and that was the action of a group of companions as we already mentioned, acting on the generality of the Book and Sunnah. The same thing applies to helping the oppressed, without any disagreement, for the one who is able, whether the oppressed is a Muslim or non-Muslim. As for responding to the sneezer (tashmīt al-ʿāṭis), it has been established from the texts that the Prophet would respond to Jews when they would sneeze, saying, “May God guide you and put you in a good state (yuṣliḥ bālakum).” Likewise for accepting invitations, and a Jew invited the Prophet ﷺ for eating and he accepted it.

Ibn ʿAbd al-Barr said, “al-Barrāʾ stated: ‘The Messenger of God ﷺ commanded us to do seven things.’ He then mentioned among it accepting invitations, as well as other things that are communal obligations, while others are obligations by the Sunnah like accepting invitations.”[84]

al-Maghribī stated: “ “The Prophet ﷺ ordered us to do seven things:”, some of which are obligations and others constitute preferable actions. “Visiting the sick”, and it’s preferable—even if he’s a non-Muslim citizen; “Following funeral processions”, which is also preferable, however, if no one is found to carry the task of burying the dead then it becomes thereafter a communal obligation; “Replying to the sneezer”, which is a communal Sunnah, if he praises God; “Helping those who vow to fulfill it”, which is preferable; And “helping the oppressed”, even if he’s a non-Muslim citizen, which is an obligation for the one who is able.”[85]

The Shāfiʿī Ibn ʿAllān explained, “He said: “The Prophet of God ﷺ commanded us to do seven things, and forbade us to do seven things: he ordered us to visit the sick,” which is preferable in all times, it’s only disliked if it will cause distress to the sick; “following funeral processions,” meaning to accompany it until the end of the burial; “replying to the sneezer (tashmīt al-ʿāṭis),” if he praises God Almighty, and the order in these three ones is one of preferance (nadb); “to help those who vow to fulfill it,” by the very way that was vowed to you, or like ‘By [God] you’ll make so-and-so’, and so he makes it if there’s no prohibition, such that he doesn’t break it in the first case, and doesn’t violate it in the second; “helping the oppressed,” even if it’s a non-Muslim citizen, it’s obligatory for anyone who is able to prevent the oppression from the oppressor, either through his actions or his tongue. And this draws back from enjoining good and forbidding evil. And this is sometimes an individual obligation, and a communal obligation in other cases.”[86]

Ibn Ḥajar stated, “Ibn Daqīq al-ʿĪd said, ‘If we look at the statements of some linguists that tashmīt constitutes a supplication for good, then non-Muslims entered in that command. However, if we look at those who restricted tashmīt to mercy, then they aren’t included in that command.’ He added, ‘And perhaps those who restricted tashmīt to mercy did so based on the most evident [meaning], since it’s a restriction to the status of the word in the language.’ I say: That analysis was done based on linguistics, and as for revelation the ḥadīth of Abū Mūsā indicates that they enter in the totality of the command of responding to the sneezer. But they have a specific reply which is to supplicate for them with guidance and being in a good state. And there’s no prohibition from that, unlike with Muslims who are the recipients of supplications for mercy in contrast to non-Mulims.”[87]

As for following funeral processions, we note here that the Messenger ﷺ ordered ʿAlī to bury his [non-Muslim] father when he died. al-ʿAynī stated, “Based on this ḥadīth our [Ḥanafī] scholars argued that if a non-Muslim kin of a Muslim died, then he should wash his body then bury him. The author of al-Hidāyah said: ‘If a non-Muslim dies and he has a Muslim guardian [then he ought to] wash him, wrap his body with a clean white shroud (takfīn), and then bury him. And that’s what he ordered ʿAlī with regards to his dad Abū Ṭālib’.”[88]

These exegetes of the ḥadīth, from the Righteous Forebears to the successors, maintained the generality of these ḥadīths for the Muslim and non-Muslim in some of these commands, and it didn’t pose a problem for them. The conclusion from their collective statements is that it’s general in spreading the greeting of peace, maintaining the ties of kinship, feeding the hungry, returning the greeting of peace, helping the oppressed, visiting the sick, responding to the sneezer, and accepting invitations. Among these actions are those that are mandatory on Muslims with regards to non-Muslims, such as helping the oppressed for the one who is able, upholdings ties of kinship with parents and others, greeting back with peace, responding to the sneezer if he praises God. Others constitute examples of preferable actions, such as spreading greetings of peace, feeding the hungry, accepting invitations, and following funeral processions if the intention was to bury the dead, and it becomes obligatory if there’s no one else to conduct that task.

The same thing applies to helping those who vow to fulfill it, whose meaning is rational and the intention from it is to honor the oaths in God’s name. The correct view is thus its generality, as Ibn al-Jawzī explains, ‘As for helping those who vow to honor it, it’s due to two meanings: One of which is to glorify God, the second of which is so that the vower doesn’t break it.’[89] al-ʿAynī stated, ‘The fifth obligation: in helping those who vow to fulfill it, which is specific to that which is permissible. And it’s among the good manners. However, if leaving it would lead to a public interest (maṣlaḥa) then [it isn’t binding].’[90]

And it’s established that good manners should be enjoined with both Muslims and non-Muslims, as is evident in the ḥadīth, “I was sent to perfect righteous—or honourable—manners (ṣāliḥ - aw makārim - al-akhlāq)”.[91]

  III. SHOWING THE CONTEXT OF SUHAYL’S ḤADĪTH AND UNCOVERING ITS FLAW:

All these reports from these companions—Ibn Masʿūd, Abū Umāmah, Faḍālah b. ʿUbayd and Abū al-Dardāʾ—indicate that they understood those Qurʾānic and Prophetic texts to be general, and they weren’t restricted in their opinion. Neither al-Nawawī, nor the Ḥāfiẓ in his al-Fatḥ, nor Ibn ʿAbd al-Barr, nor anyone else mentioned an evidence for restricting the generality of these texts except the ḥadīth of Suhayl from his dad!

And three flaws can be found

وقد اجتمع فيه ثلاث علل تستوجب التثبت فيه:

Firstly: Suhayl b. Abī Ṣāliḥ became weak in his retention (hifẓ), as al-Bukhārī said. For this specific reason he avoided recorse to him in argumentation in his Ṣaḥīḥ! And Abū Ḥātim and Yaḥyā b. Maʿīn said: ‘He’s unfit for use as proof.’

Secondly: He was its sole narrator, Ibn ʿAdī , and no one other than Suhayl related the wording: “make him take the narrowest path of the road.”

الثانية: أنه تفرد به، ولم يتابع على روايته هذه، لا عن أبيه، ولا عن أبي هريرة، حتى ذكر حديثه هذا ابن عدي في الكامل، ولم يرو أحد غير سهيل لفظة(واضطروهم إلى أضيق الطريق)!

Thirdly: His inconsistency in his narration has been established: sometimes he says ‘the polytheists’, other times ‘People of the Book’, and in other instances ‘the Jews’. As such it’s necessary to verify which version is correct .

الثالثة:أنه ثبت اضطرابه في روايته، فتارة يقول(المشركين)، وتارة(أهل الكتاب)، وتارة (اليهود)، فوجب التثبت منه.

So did Suhayl hear from his dad from Abū Hurayrah the ḥadīth with the wording “if you meet the Jews then don’t initiate the greeting of peace”, or he heard it with the wording “if you meet the People of the Book”, or “if you meet the polytheists”, or “don’t initiate the greeting of peace” Did Suhayl narrate it ? Or did he make analogy between the Jews and the People of the Book? Or an analogy between the polytheists and People of the Book? Or did the independent reasoning originate from his dad by making analogy between the Christians in their churches with the Jews of Banū Qurayẓah when the Prophet forbade initiating the greeting of peace with them, and without taking into consideration the difference between combatants and others?

فهل سمع سهيل من أبيه عن أبي هريرة الحديث بلفظ (إذا لقيتم اليهود فلا تبدؤوهم بالسلام) أم سمعه بلفظ(إذا لقيتم أهل الكتاب) أم (إذا لقيتم المشركين) أم (لا تبدؤوهم بالسلام)على الاضمار لقوم معهودين للمخاطبين؟ وهل رواها سهيل بالمعنى واجتهد فيها؟ أم قاس على اليهود أهل الكتاب؟ أم قاس المشركين على أهل الكتاب؟ أم كان الاجتهاد من أبيه في قياس النصارى في الصوامع على يهود بني قريضة حين منع النبي صلى الله عليه وسلم من ابتدائهم بالسلام دون مراعاة الفرق بين المحاربين وغيرهم؟

Each of these wordings doesn’t constitute an independent ḥadīth from

idhtirab = inconsistency

What further confirms that this is an impactful difference is the fact that Muslim in his Ṣaḥīḥ, Aḥmad in his Musnad, as well as others warned about the disagreement where some say ‘the Jews’, others ‘People of the Book’, while others ‘the polytheists.’

ومما يؤكد أنه خلاف مؤثر كون مسلم في صحيحه وأحمد في مسنده وغيرهما نبهوا على الخلاف بين أصحاب سهيل بعضهم يقول(اليهود) وبعضهم(أهل الكتاب) وبعضهم (المشركين).

If that’s established, then the ruling will be different depending on the wording. If you consider the transmission mentioning the Jews to be the most reliable—without taking a look at the other witness narrations (shawāhid)—then the ruling in such case would be restricted to Jews, and other groups may be included by analogy. However, one can disagree with that by arguing that the general texts on spreading greetings of peace remain in its generality, and the Jews are excluded because of this ḥadīth that is specific to them due to their harm and hostility towards the Prophet ﷺ and to Muslims, they thus they deserved such ruling.

However, if the correct wording was the one containing ‘the polytheists’, then the prohibition from initiating the greeting of peace with non-Muslims becomes general, without regards to their creed and religion.

Invoking that Abī Ṣāliḥ, the father of Suhayl, would say ‘don’t initiate the greeting of peace with them’ when he would pass nearby the churches of the Christians, doesn’t constitute a correct argument. Since this could’ve been the result of his own independent reasoning, and what’s required is the wording of his narration—and not his opinion. And his narration has only been transmitted via his son, Suhayl, and he was inconsistent in its respect. And what confirms this is the statements of the narrators from him such as Jarīr b. ʿAbd al-Ḥamīd ‘who said: ’, and the statement of Zuhayr b. Muʿāwiyah when he was asked by ʿAlī b. al-Jaʿd about the wording of Suhayl: ‘I said to Zuhayl: The Jews and the Christians? He replied: the polytheists’.

All these authentic ḥadīths from these companions came in the context of the depart of the Prophet ﷺ to besiege Banū Qurayẓah after their treason in the battle of the trenches (al-Khunduq), so he ordered the companions to not initiate the greeting of peace with them, and to make them take the narrowest path of the road, since they’re combatants. Initiating the greeting of peace with them would constitute a guarantee of safety (amān) to them. These ḥadīths explain the generality and abbreviation that was present in the ḥadīth of Abū Hurayrah, who didn’t witness that event since he only converted to Islām two lunar years after, precisely in 7 A.H. Whereas the besiege of Banū Qurayẓah was in 5 A.H, after the battle of the trenches. This ḥadīth isn’t absolute nor general on polytheists and People of the Book, and not even on all Jews. Rather, it’s in the case of a war with combatants, so non-Muslims under our protection, those with whom there’s a treaty, and peaceful polytheists aren’t encompassed in that command.

Isḥāq b. Rāhawayh explained this ḥadīth to mean the prohibition of giving a guarantee of safety (amān) to those Jews when the Prophet ﷺ marched to besiege them, since they’re combatants. He said: “If you have a need with one, then you may initiate the greeting of peace with them. And the meaning of the Prophet’s saying “don’t initiate the greeting of peace with them” is when he feared that it would constitute a guarantee of safety (amān) while he was marching [to fight] those Jews.”[106]

This was the same explanation given by Shaykh al-Islām Ibn Taymiyyah concerning the context of the ḥadīth prohibiting initiating the greeting of peace with Jews, as related by Ibn Qayyim, “As for the statement of the Prophet ﷺ, “don’t initiate the greeting of peace with them”, then this was when he marched to fight them, and they’re the Jews of Qurayẓah. So he ordered that they don’t initiate the greeting of peace, since it constitutes a guarantee of safety (amān), while he went to fight them. I heard our Shaykh say that.”[107]

Ibn Qayyim said: “It was said that this was in a specific situation in which they marched to Banū Qurayẓah stating “don’t initiate the greeting of peace with them”, as such, is it a general command for all people of the convenant? Or is it rather specific to a similar situation? This is a spot for consideration, Muslim narrated in his Ṣaḥīḥ from the ḥadīth of Abū Hurayrah that the Prophet ﷺ said, “don’t initiate the greeting of peace with Christians or Jews. And when you meet any of them in the road, then make him take its narrowest path.” What is apparent is that it’s a general ruling. Both the Righteous Forebears and the successors differed on this matter, many of them held that we don’t initiate the greeting of peace with them. Others however held that it was permissible to initiate it, just like for greeting them back with peace: This opinion was ascribed to Ibn ʿAbbās, Abū Umāmah, Ibn Muḥayrīz, and it’s a position in the school of al-Shāfiʿī—may God have mercy on him—but the one who holds this view specified that only ‘peace be upon you’ is said to him, without mentioning the mercy part and in the singular form. A group of other scholars said that it’s permissible to initiate the greeting of peace with non-Muslims if it serves a beneficial purpose or need, or if he fears he will be harmed by them, or if he has family relations with them, or for another reason for which it’s required. This was narrated by Ibrāhīm al-Nakhaʿī and ʿAlqamah. al-Awzāʿī declared: ‘If you initiate the greeting of peace, then so did the righteous; and if you don’t initiate it, then so did the righteous.’ They also differed concerning the obligation of returning the greeting, with the majority maintaining that it’s obligatory—and this is the correct view. And a group said: It’s not obligatory to return the greeting with them, just as it isn’t obligatory with the people of heresy (ahl al-bidʿah). But the correct view is the first one: the difference is that we’re commanded to forsake the people of heresy so as to deter and warn them [from their heresy], in contrast to the non-Muslims under our protection.”[108]

What Isḥāq b. Rāhawayh, Shaykh al-Islām Ibn Taymiyyah and Ibn Qayyim mentioned regarding the context of this ḥadīth is the correct view as proven by the other authentic narrations.

al-Bukhārī included in a chapter titled ‘Initiating the greeting of peace in a gathering with both Muslims and polytheists’ the ḥadīth of Usāmah b. Go back to your mount (or house,) and if anyone of us comes to you, then preach to him’.”[109]

And al-Bayhaqī narrated it with the wording: “The Prophet ﷺ passed by a gathering with Muslims, Jews and polytheists, and he greeted all of them with peace.” al-Bayhaqī notes that this was included in the two Ṣaḥīḥs.[110] It’s also included in the Muṣannaf of ʿAbd al-Razzāq, but without the wording ‘all of them.’[111]

And this is a practical Sunnah that confirms the generality of the Qurʾānic verses and the permissibility of initiating the greeting of peace with non-Muslims. Stating that the Prophet’s ﷺ greeting of peace was only addressed towards those Muslims that were present [in the gathering] requires consideration, for it’s not supported by the apparent meaning of the ḥadīth: what is evident is that the gathering was led by ʿAbd Allāh b. Ubay b. Salūl, and he’s their chief and leader, and [Jews] were the majority there. In addition, there’s an even more clear proof for that: in the fact that the Prophet ﷺ invited them to Islām, and also recited to them verses from the Qurʾān, an indication that he only stopped by and greeted them with peace to invite them to Islām, and not so that he sits in their gathering. All those who took the burden of making reconciling interpretations were only led to do so because of Suhayl’s ḥadīth from his dad “don’t initiate the greeting of peace with polytheists.”[112]

This practical Sunnah is also reinforced by the ḥadīth of Abū Hurayrah in the Ṣaḥīḥayn that God told Ādam “to greet that group, and that was a party of angels sitting there, and listen to the response that they give him, for it would form his greeting and that of his offspring. He then went away and said: ‘Peace be upon you!’ They (the angels) said: ‘May there be peace upon you and the Mercy of God’, and they made an addition of ‘Mercy of God’.”[113]

And this is explicitly clear on that this particular greeting is general, the greeting of Adam and his children, that his offspring would use to greet one another.

  IV. ḤADĪTHS ON GREETING BACK WITH PEACE NON-MUSLIMS:

There came a bunch of ḥadīths, other than those of Abū Baṣrah al-Ghifārī and Abū ʿAbd al-Raḥmān al-Juhanī, on merely greeting back the Jews with ‘and upon you’ if they initiate the greeting of peace, among which:

  1. The ḥadīth of Ibn ʿUmar in the Ṣaḥīḥayn which we already described, and it was narrated as well by Aḥmad who said: Sufyān (Ibn ʿUyaynah) narrated, I heard from Ibn Dīnār, from Ibn ʿUmar, from the Prophet ﷺ: “If the Jew greets you stating ‘Death be upon you!’, then say, ‘and upon you!’ ”, and in another version: “If the Jews greet you then say, ‘and upon you!’, for they state ‘Death be upon you!’.”[118]
  2. The ḥadīth of the wife of the Prophet ﷺ ʿĀʾishah—may God be pleased with her—who stated: A group of Jews came to God’s Messenger ﷺ and said, ‘Death be upon you’, and I understood it and said to them, “And death and curse be upon you.” God’s Messenger ﷺ said, “Be calm! O ʿĀʾishah, for God loves kindness and leniency (rifq) in all matters.” I said. “O God’s Messenger! Haven’t you heard what they have said?” God’s Messenger ﷺ responded, “I have (already) said ‘and upon you!’.”[119]
    And in another version: That [some] Jews came to the Prophet ﷺ and said, ‘Death be upon you’, and so ʿĀʾishah stated, “And upon you and may God curse you and shower His wrath upon you!” He (the Prophet) responded: “Be calm! O ʿĀʾishah, you should be endowed with kindness and leniency, and beware of harshness and saying bad words.” She said, “Haven’t you heard what they have said?” He replied, “Didn’t you hear what I said? I have returned their statement to them, and my invocation against them will be accepted but theirs against me won’t be accepted.”[120]
  3. The ḥadīth of Jābir b. ʿAbd Allāh: Some Jewish people greeted the Messenger of God ﷺ by saying ‘Death be upon you O Abā al-Qāsim.’ He responded “and upon you”, and ʿĀʾishah became angry and asked: “Didn’t you hear what they said?” He responded, “Yes I did hear, I have returned their statement to them, and my invocation against them will be accepted but theirs against me won’t be accepted.”[121]
  4. The ḥadīth of Anas b. Mālik: A Jew passed nearby the Messenger of God ﷺ and said ‘Death be upon you.’ He responded “and upon you”, and then said (to his companions), “Do you know what he stated? He said ‘Death be upon you’.” They asked the Messenger of God ﷺ, “Shall we kill him?” He responded, “No! When the People of the Book greet you then respond saying ‘and upon you’.”[122]
    And in another narration from Anas: A Jew passed nearby the Prophet ﷺ and his companions and said ‘Death be upon you!’ Thereafter the companions greeted him back, and so the Prophet ﷺ noted, “he said ‘Death be upon you’.” And so the Jew was taken and was brought and he confessed. The Prophet ﷺ said, “Reply back to them with what they said.”[123] And in another version “Reply back to him with what he said.”[124] Meaning that you should merely do that, and don’t kill him as some companions wanted.

All these authentic ḥadīths contain only the permission of replying back with the same by saying ‘and upon you!’—meaning whatever you say be upon you, whether it’s good or bad. And the ruling is justified in them where he said: “If the Jew greets you stating ‘Death be upon you!’, then say, ‘and upon you!’ ”, and in another version: “If the Jews greet you then say, ‘and upon you!’, for they state ‘Death be upon you!’.” And the purpose of the fāʾ (ف) is to ascribe the mentioned ruling upon its cause, and there’s a clear justification for this ruling in the ḥadīth “for they state ‘Death be upon you!’.” Moreover, these ḥadīths contain a prohibition on using bad and harsh words, which constitutes of making further additions when replying back, as ʿĀʾishah did when she said, ‘Death and disgrace be upon you’ and ‘O brothers of monkeys and pigs...’ He also didn’t permit the companions to kill or harm those who said that from the People of the Book, and he commanded that they be treated kindly!

Neither generality nor absoluteness are implied in these ḥadīths, rather they’re specific to those who greeted Muslims with expressions of disparagement or supplications for evil, in which case he merely replies back with ‘and upon you’, as a way of reciprocity and treating others in a likewise manner without having recorse to bad and harsh words. And this is among the noble and good manners of Islām. As for the one who greets with peace or who makes a good greeting, then it’s obligatory to greet back with a better or similar greeting. And it shouldn’t be argued based on these ḥadīths that one should merely greet back with ‘and upon you’ as was held by the majority, since this contradicts the generality of the verse “But when you [believers] are offered a greeting, respond with a better one, or at least return it.”[125]

What is obligatory by the Book is to greet back with sonething better or similar, and not merely using the least such as saying ‘and upon you’ since that’s restricted to those who say ‘Death be upon you!’ and similar expressions. And this doesn’t even constitute a greeting, so it isn’t covered by the generality of the verse. The reason being is that greetings are a supplication for life, however al-Sām (death) is a supplication for evil, so one merely replies back by saying ‘and upon you’—meaning whatever you say.

Ibn Jarīr al-Ṭabarī stated, “‘But when you [believers] are offered a greeting’: if it’s supplicated for you for longevity, survival and safety; ‘respond with a better one, or at least return it’, He says: then supplicate for the one who made that with something better than what was supplicated for you; ‘or at least return it’: or give back his greeting.”[126]

Some narrators abbreviated this ḥadīth of Anas, so they transmitted its shorter version without mentioning the full story, distorting thereby its meaning:

It was narrated by Aḥmad: Hushaym narrated, ʿUbayd Allāh b. Abū Bakr b. Anas informed us, from his grandfather Anas b. Mālik who said: The Messenger of God ﷺ stated: “If the People of the Book greet you, then reply using ‘and upon you’.”[127]

And it was related by Aḥmad, Yaḥyā narrated, from Shuʿbah, Qatādah and Ibn Jaʿfar narrated, saying: Shuʿbah narrated stating: I heard Qatādah from Anas who said: the companions of the Prophet ﷺ asked him, ‘the People of the Book initiate the greeting of peace with us, so how do we respond back?’ He answered: “reply with ‘and upon you’.” And there’s a similar narration from Ḥajjāj. Shuʿbah stated: I didn’t ask Qatādah whether he heard this specific ḥadīth from Anas.[128]

And Qatādah is famous for being an obfuscator in transmission (mudallis).[129] And Shuʿbah didn’t stop him on whether he received the report directly from his source, as if he’s doubting whether he heard this ḥadīth in this particular wording that was abbreviated from Anas, since its wording is general, as if the companions were asking about the ruling of replying with the People of the Book in general, and he ordered them to not use anything more than ‘and upon you’. Despite that he stated it with regards to those who say ‘Death be upon you’, and not with respect to everyone who greeted with peace from the People of the Book as this problematic wording purports!

For this reason, neither al-Bukhārī nor Muslim included this ḥadīth from a path via Qatādah from Anas despite his great status!

It was also related by Aḥmad: that Ismāʿīl b. ʿUlayyah narrated, that Ibn ʿAwn narrated, from Ḥamīd b. Zādawayh who said: Anas b. Mālik stated: “We were prohibited—or he said: ordered that we don’t—from [returning the greeting of peace] to People of the Book with anything more than ‘and upon you’.”[130]

These narrations are abbreviated from the longer narration from Anas in the story of the Jew who greeted with ‘Death be upon you’, after which some of the companions wanted to attack and kill him, and the Prophet forbade them from that, and ordered that they do nothing other than state ‘and upon you’. In other words, that they don’t surpass his aggression and say bad words, and limit themselves to that (i.e. ‘and upon you’). This was abbreviated by some narrators, and was set apart from the context of the story, and it became “We were ordered that we don’t [return the greeting of peace] to People of the Book with anything more than ‘and upon you’.” where it was understood that he forbade them from adding ‘Peace’ before saying ‘upon you’, or the addition of ‘the Mercy and Blessings of God’, despite that he forbade them from making additions containing bad words and insults, as ʿĀʾishah did, and he ordered that they merely use ‘and upon you’.

  V. ELUCIDATING THE CORRECT VIEW:

What emerges as the correct view from the collection of all these ḥadīths is that there’s no prohibition from initiating the greeting of peace with non-Muslims, except in only one circumstance: in the case of war, so that it doesn’t constitute a guarantee of safety (amān) with all its legal consequences. Other than that, it’s preferable to spread greetings of peace to anyone, be it a Muslim or non-Muslim, as has been related from most companions—may God be pleased with them—who had a say in this issue.

al-Qurṭubī argued[131] that the apparent meaning from the Qurʾān indicates the permissibility of initiating the greeting of peace with them based on the generality of the texts, and he said: “The permissibility of initiating the greeting of peace with non-Muslims was stated by some of the Righteous Forebears (Salaf). Ibn ʿUyaynah replied in the affirmative when asked whether initiating the greeting of peace with non-Muslims was permissible, and he cited the verse, “God does not forbid you to deal kindly and justly with anyone who has not fought you for your faith or driven you out of your homes: God loves the just”, and “You have a good example in Abraham”, and he said to his [non-Muslim] dad: “Peace be with you”. I say: What is apparent from the verse is what Sufyān Ibn ʿUyaynah explained.”[132]

Furthermore, al-Qurṭubī didn’t find anything that would repulse that opinion except the ḥadīth of Suhayl, he said, “Concerning this matter, there are two authentic ḥadīths: Abū Hurayrah narrated that the Prophet ﷺ said, “don’t initiate the greeting of peace with Christians and Jews. And when you meet any of them in the road, then make him take its narrowest path”, which was included by al-Bukhārī and Muslim (that’s what al-Qurṭubī said, and it’s a confusion from him since al-Bukhārī didn’t include this ḥadīth). And in the Ṣaḥīḥayn, from Usāmah b. Zayd: “the Prophet ﷺ ... passed by a gathering in which there were Muslims, pagan idolators and Jews, and among them there was ʿAbd Allāh b. Ubay b. Salūl, and there was ʿAbd Allāh b. Rawāḥah too. When a cloud of dust raised by the animal covered that gathering, ʿAbd Allāh b. Ubay covered his nose with his sheet and said, ‘Don’t cover us with dust.’ The Prophet ﷺ greeted them with peace.” The first one implies not initiating the greeting of peace with them, since that’s a form of ikrām and non-Muslims aren’t its recipients, whereas the second ḥadīth allows it. al-Ṭabarī said: ‘What Usāmah narrated doesn’t contradict the ḥadīth of Abū Hurayrah, for the latter implies generality, whereas the meaning of the narration of Usāmah is restrictive.’ al-Nakhaʿī said: ‘If you have some need with a Jew or Christian then initiate the greeting of peace with them.’ As such, the ḥadīth of Abū Hurayrah “don’t initiate the greeting of peace with them” applies only if there’s no reason that would lead one to initiate the greeting of peace, such as when fulfilling a right towards them, or a need with them, or by right of companionship, or neighbor, or in a travel. al-Ṭabarī said: ‘It was narrated from the Righteous Forebears (Salaf) that they used to initiate the greeting of peace with People of the Book, and this is exemplified by the act of Ibn Masʿūd with the merchants who accompagnied him in the road. ʿAlqamah said to him: ‘O father of ʿAbd al-Raḥmān! Isn’t it disliked to initiate the greeting of peace with them?’ And he replied: ‘Yes, but it’s the right of companionship’.’[133] al-Awzāʿī declared when he was asked about a Muslim who initiates the greeting of peace with a non-Muslim: ‘If you initiate the greeting of peace, then so did the righteous before you; and if you don’t initiate it, then so did the righteous before you.’ It was narrated from al-Ḥassan al-Baṣrī that he said, ‘If you pass by a gathering with both Muslims and non-Muslims, then initiate the greeting of peace with them’.”[134]

In addition, one should greet back with peace polytheists according to the correct view, which is the majority opnion. al-Qurṭubī said: “There was some difference of opinion concerning greeting back non-Muslim citizens with the greeting of peace: is it obligatory just like with greeting back Muslims? That was the opinion of Ibn ʿAbbās, al-Shaʿbiy and Qatādah, based on the generality of the verse (Q.4:86), and the order to greet them back in the authentic Sunnah.”[135]

Furthermore, one shouldn’t merely greet them back with the expression ‘and upon you’ except in two circumstances:

First: In the case of war, where they aren’t responded back—if they initiate it—with ‘and peace be upon you’, since it constitutes a guarantee of safety (amān) that has legal consequences, and that contradicts the state of war with them. For this reason the Prophet ﷺ said in the day of the expedition to Banū Qurayẓah, “I’m departing to Jews, so to whoever went with me: don’t initiate the greeting of peace with them. If however they greet you with peace then say, ‘and upon you’. So when we came to them they greeted us with peace, and we said, ‘and upon you’.”

And this is what’s indicated by the ḥadīth of Abū Baṣrah and Abū ʿAbd al-Raḥmān al-Juhanī, as well as the lengthy and detailed ḥadīth of al-Thawrī, from Ibn Dīnār, from Ibn ʿUmar, that was included by al-Bayhaqī.

So the greeting of peace isn’t initiated with them, nor given back, since it constitutes a guarantee of safety contradicting the state of war.

Secondly: If they greet with a supplication for evil such as ‘Death be upon you’, then they’re responded with the wording ‘and upon you’ as a way of reciprocity without invoking bad words—as dictated by the Prophet ﷺ in the narration of Sufyān b. ʿUyaynah, from Ibn Dīnār: “If the Jew greets you stating ‘Death be upon you!’, then say, ‘and upon you!’” And he—Sufyān or his teacher—said: “If the Jews greet you then say, ‘and upon you!’, for they state ‘Death be upon you!’.”

The more explicit narration is the one of Sufyan al-Thawrī—included by Ibn Abī Shaybah—from ʿAbd Allāh b. Dīnār, from Ibn ʿUmar who related that the Prophet ﷺ said, “If the Jews meet you saying, ‘Death be upon you’ then reply by telling them, ‘and upon you’.”

And this is what the ḥadīth of ʿĀʾishah, Jābir, Anas and ʿAbd Allāh b. ʿUmar convey.

In all other cases, if they make a good greeting, then what is obligatory by the Book is to respond with a better one—which is the preferable course of action as evidenced by the fact that it was mentioned first in verse[136]—or returning it in a similar form. This entails not merely using expressions that are lesser than it, just as is apparent from the verse: “But when you [believers] are offered a greeting, respond with a better one, or at least return it.”[137]

And this is the opinion of Ibn ʿAbbās, for he says: ‘Greet back with peace to Jews, Christians, or Zoroastrians, since God says: “But when you [believers] are offered a greeting, respond with a better one, or at least return it”.’[138]

Moreover, the generality of the verse also includes the case in which a polytheist says “may the Peace, Mercy and Blessings of God be bestowed upon you.” As such, it is obligatory to greet him back with that same expression. In addition, it was authentically narrated that Ibn ʿAbbās said, “If Pharaoh had said to me, ‘May God bless you,’ I would have said, ‘And you too.’ But Pharaoh is dead.”[139]

al-Bukhārī included it in al-Adab al-Mufrad in the chapter ‘How to supplicate for a non-Muslim under our protection.’ The aim of Ibn ʿAbbās was to show the permissibility of supplicating for non-Muslims with blessings and good, as was related from Ibn ʿUmar and Uqbah b. ʿĀmir that they supplicated for a non-Muslim citizen stating ‘may God give you much wealth and many children’, which is one of the many worldly blessings.

Ibn Qayyim stated, “How do we greet them back if it’s established that they said to us ‘peace be upon you’? If it’s sure without doubt to the hearer that the non-Muslim citizen said to him, ‘peace be upon you’, then should he greet him back with ‘and peace be upon you’ or just ‘and upon you’? What the evidence from the Sharīʿah and its principles dictate is that he shall be responded with ‘and peace be upon you’. For this classifies as justice, and God ordains justice and kindness, and He said: “But when you [believers] are offered a greeting, respond with a better one, or at least return it”, so He preferred the best, and commanded justice. And this in no way contradicts the other ḥadīths on this matter. For he ordered to merely greet back with ‘and upon you!’ based on the mentioned cause, namely the expression they used in their greetings, as he indicated in the ḥadīth of ʿĀʾishah—may God be pleased with her—when he said, “Haven’t you seen that I said ‘and upon you’ when they stated ‘Death be upon you’?” He then added, “If the People of the Book greet you then respond saying, ‘and upon you’.” And while consideration is granted to the generality of the wording, it is only granted to the generality with respect to what’s mentioned, and not with respect to what opposes it. God Almighty says: “When they come to you they greet you with words God has never used to greet you, and say inwardly, ‘Why does God not punish us for what we say?’”, so when this cause is no longer present, and the one from the People of the Book says, ‘May the Peace and Mercy of God be upon you!’, then being just in the greeting would entail greeting back with that same expression.”[140]

Supplication for non-Muslims with good, prosperity, and blessings is permissible, as indicated by the verse concerning parents—even if they’re polytheists, “And lower your wing in humility towards them in kindness and say, ‘Lord, have mercy on them, just as they cared for me when I was little’.”[141]

al-Ṭabarī said: “And a group of scholars interpreted the verse: “It is not fitting for the Prophet and the believers to ask forgiveness for the idolaters—even if they’re related to them ...”[142] that God prohibited asking forgiveness for idolaters after their death, as the verse continues: “after having been shown that they are the inhabitants of the Blaze”. In fact, supplicating them with the wording ‘May God guide you and put you in a good state (yuṣliḥ bālakum)’ may be better than with ‘May God have mercy on you’ or ‘May God forgive you.’ Ibn ʿAbd al-Barr said, “And al-Ṭaḥāwī chose the expression ‘May God guide you and put you in a good state’ since it’s better than his greeting—i.e. the statement to the sneezer ‘May God have mercy on you’ or ‘May God forgive you’—since the state of the one who is guided and made himself into a good state is greater than the one who was forgiven.”[148]

al-Ḥāfiẓ Ibn Ḥajar said, “This [opinion] was held by al-Ṭaḥāwī from the Ḥanafīs, and used as evidence the verse, “But when you [believers] are offered a greeting, respond with a better one ...”. He continued: ‘The one who replies [to the sneezer] saying ‘May God forgive us and you’ doesn’t surpass the meaning of the statement ‘May God have mercy on you’ for the sneezer, since forgiveness is to hide (satr) the sin, and mercy is to leave the punishment for it. This is in contrast to supplicating for him with guidance and being in a good state whose meaning is that he’ll be free of making sins and in a good state, so it’s better than the first one, thus it’s the preferred [course of action]’.”

What is implied is that greeting back a polytheist who said ‘May the Peace, Blessings and Mercy of God be upon you!’ requires stating back: ‘And may the Peace, Blessings and Mercy of God be upon you!’ And it doesn’t constitute a sound argument to invoke the ḥadīth of the Jews who were sneezing in the presence of the Prophet ﷺ, since that’s a practical Sunnah with regards to replying to the non-Muslim sneezers, by supplicating for them with guidance and being in a good state. And that’s the very same supplication that a Muslim sneezer would say to a Muslim if he replied back to him by saying ‘May God forgive you’, in which case [the sneezer] says: ‘May God guide you and put you in a good state’. As such, it’s mistaken to make an analogy with the greeting that is obligatory by the Book, by responding with a better one or returning it.

And Suhayl isn’t the type of narrators from which such a lack of corrobation can be accepted, to the point that Abū Ḥātim and Yaḥyā b. Maʿīn said: ‘He’s unfit for use as proof.’ And al-Bukhārī didn’t include him And Ibn ʿAdī mentioned this , especially the wording ‘and when you meet any of them in the road, then make him take its narrowest path’ in this general and absolute sense.
  5. 3 ـ كما قام الباحث بالبحث عن متابعاته وشواهده، فوقف فيه على نكارة وتفرد، فلم يتابع سهيل أحد على روايته لا عن أبيه ولا عن أبي هريرة ولا عن النبي صلى الله عليه وسلم بهذا اللفظ العام المطلق، وليس مثله من يقبل منه هذا التفرد، حتى قال أبو حاتم ويحيى بن معين(لا يحتج به)، ولم يخرج له البخاري احتجاجا، وقد ذكر ابن عدي هذا الحديث في غرائب حديثه ومناكيره، خاصة لفظ(اضطروهم إلى أضيق الطريق) هكذا على سبيل العموم والإطلاق.

  6. The researcher also gathered witness reports (shawāhid) of this narration—the ḥadīths of Abū Baṣrah, al-Juhanī and Ibn ʿUmar—and found that all of them were centered around a long story that explains the abbreviation in Suhayl’s ḥadīth. It also conformed with it in the wording “if you meet the Jews, then don’t initiate the greeting of peace with them”, but it added as well the mention of the story as well as “we’re marching tomorrow”, and “If however they greet you with peace then say, ‘and upon you’.”

This part of Suhayl’s ḥadīth is authentic, and it’s the one that conforms with the other ḥadīths. And it was established that the wordings mentioning ‘the polytheists’ and ‘People of the Book’ aren’t authentic, and that they’re a result of an alteration from Suhayl. And it hasn’t been corroborated even by a single witness report (shāhid). His only narration that conformed with other ḥadīths was the one transmitted from him by Wakīʿ from al-Thawrī with the wording mentioning ‘the Jews.’

As such, it has been established that this ḥadīth came in the context of a story that elucidates its meaning, and that it isn’t general, nor absolute. Furthermore, Suhayl shortned it causing thereby a disruption, or he forgot, or he and his dade made independent reasoning by making analogy between the People of the Book and Christians that he used to meet in Syria, and between the Jews that were mentioned in the ḥadīth. Or he may have taken it to be general, again, using his own independent reasoning. On the other hand, Abū Baṣrah al-Ghifārī, Abū ʿAbd al-Raḥmān al-Juhanī and ʿAbd Allāh b. ʿUmar narrated it in the context of the Prophet’s ﷺ march to besiege Banū Qurayẓah, in which he prohibited his companions from initiating the greeting of peace with them, and ordered that they merely greet back with “and upon you,” such that it doesn’t constitute a guarantee of safety (amān) that would thus forbid fighting them. He also commanded that they force them to take the narrowest path of the road for the very same reason—which is that he came to fight them.

Fourthly: In addition, there came many general authentic ḥadīths from a number of companions—among them al-Barāʾ b. ʿĀzib, ʿAbd Allāh b. Salām, ʿAbd Allāh b. ʿAmrū, and Abū Umāmah al-Bāhilīy—that command spreading the greeting of peace to the whole world, with those you know and those you don’t know, and it is in accordance with the apparent meaning of Qurʾānic verses.

Fifthly: The practical Sunnah of the Prophet ﷺ is in accordance with his sayings: It was authenticatly narrated that he came to a gathering in which there were Muslims, pagan idolators and Jews, and he greeted them all with peace, then he invited them to God and also recited to them verses from the Qurʾān.

Sixthly: It was established from a number of companions such as Ibn Masʿūd, Abū Umāmah al-Bāhilīy, and narrated from Faḍālah b. ʿUbayd, Abū al-Dardāʾ and Ibn ʿAbbās that they were initiating the greeting of peace with non-Muslims, or they greeted with peace everyone they encountered be it a Muslim or non-Muslim, basing this on the generality of the Qurʾānic and Prophetic texts. And among those who held the permissibility of this are al-Nakhaʿī, Ibn ʿUyaynah and al-Awzāʿī. Moreover, al-Qurṭubī maintained that this was indeed the apparent meaning from the Qurʾānic texts, if not for the ḥadīth of Suhayl. Isḥāq b. Rāhawayh and al-Ṭabarī agreed with them if there was a reason that required initiating the greeting of peace, such as in a companionship, or neighborhood, or travel, or in the case of a need, or other similar reasons. The correct view however is its absolute permissibility because of the generality of the evidence. The prohibition of initiating the greeting of peace with them concerns only the case of war.

Seventhly: What is established concerning greeting back anyone—whether Muslim or non-Muslim—is to greet with something better, or similar, just as the generality of this verse suggests, “But when you [believers] are offered a greeting, respond with a better one, or at least return it.”

Heightly: Greeting back with the mere expression “and upon you” is restricted to only two circumstances: The first one concerns the case of war, such that not uttering peace won’t constitute a guarantee of safety (amān). The second one, in the case of harm and supplication for evil in the greeting, in which case one greets back with the mere expression “and upon you”, with no other additions. And this is part of Islām’s good manners, and its guidance and kindness to the creation.

Ninthly: Also if a non-Muslim greets a Muslim with the expression “may the Peace, Mercy and Blessings of God be bestowed upon you” then it’s necessary to return one’s greeting with that same expression, as argued by Ibn Qayyim, based on the Qurʾānic texts to this effect.

Tenthly: These Qurʾānic and Prophetic Manners are among the virtues of Islām and its mercy to mankind, and kindness that extends to all creation, and among the perfection of the Sharīʿah that came with justice, equity, kindness, and mercy in organizing the relation between Muslims and others.
